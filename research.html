Research

What I study, and why

My work sits at the intersection of geodynamics and planetary habitability — using numerical modeling to understand how the deep interiors of Earth and other planetary bodies evolve, and how that evolution is coupled to what happens at the surface.

Active projects

Current research

01

Long-term survival of LLVPs

Large Low-Velocity Provinces (LLVPs) are massive structures at the base of Earth's mantle whose longevity has been hard to explain. I use mantle convection models to show that internal layering can stabilize LLVPs and allow them to persist over geologic timescales — published in Physics of the Earth and Planetary Interiors (2025).

02

Core-derived materials in mantle dynamics

Investigating how lighter materials released from Earth's core influence patterns of mantle convection, connecting core-mantle boundary chemistry to large-scale mantle dynamics.

03

Hemispheric thermal dichotomy in the lunar mantle

Modeling the striking asymmetry between the Moon's near and far side to understand what it reveals about the lunar mantle's thermal and compositional evolution — presented at EGU 2026.

04

Hydrogen and nitrogen storage in mantle coesite

Working with Prof. Sang-Heon Shim on the coupled storage of hydrogen and nitrogen in coesite, relevant to volatile cycling in Earth's mantle.

Methods & tools

How I work

I build numerical mantle convection models using CitcomS and Citcom2D, with computational work in C and Python and mapping/visualization via GMT (Generic Mapping Tools). I also have experimental experience with multi-anvil press techniques, and use COMSOL Multiphysics, Origin, and Adobe Illustrator for modeling and figure preparation.
