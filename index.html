PhD Candidate, Geological Sciences

School of Earth and Space Exploration, Arizona State University

My research focuses on understanding the structure, dynamics, and long-term evolution of Earth's deep interior using numerical modeling, with a particular interest in the lower mantle and Large Low-Velocity Provinces (LLVPs). I also investigate the interiors of other planetary bodies, including the Moon, to better understand their thermal and compositional evolution.

About

A little more about my work

I'm advised by Dr. Mingming Li in the School of Earth and Space Exploration, where I study geodynamics and planetary habitability — how the deep interiors of Earth and other planetary bodies evolve over time, and how that evolution shapes what happens at the surface. Most of my work uses numerical mantle convection models, particularly around the survival and structure of Large Low-Velocity Provinces (LLVPs) at the base of Earth's mantle.

I'm currently a fourth-year PhD candidate (started August 2022) and also serve as a Graduate Teaching Associate at ASU.

Active research

What I'm currently working on

01

Long-term survival of LLVPs

Investigating how internal layering within Large Low-Velocity Provinces allows them to persist in Earth's lower mantle over geologic time.

02

Core-derived materials in mantle dynamics

Examining the role of lighter materials released from Earth's core in shaping patterns of mantle convection.

03

Hemispheric thermal dichotomy in the lunar mantle

Modeling the thermal asymmetry between the Moon's near and far side to better understand its interior evolution.
